Overview

If a shop requires you to ship an irreplaceable harness before pricing, timelines, and exit terms are crystal clear, you should treat it as a high-risk transaction. The risk is not only money — it is loss of control over your property.

Consumer warning

Read before sending

High-friction custodial model

The primary risk is a shift in leverage after shipment: possession, undefined timelines, and an exit process that can become confrontational. Treat this as a custody-and-control transaction unless the shop provides objective testing standards, a written timeline, and a clear return policy before any irreversible work.

If your harness is altered during “inspection,” leaving may no longer be practical.

Common pattern (plain language)

  1. Ship first: property leaves your control before a fixed scope/price.
  2. Inspect internally: outcomes are described, not measured publicly.
  3. Irreversibility: early steps can make “return as received” impossible.
  4. Delay normalization: long timelines are framed as safety/quality.
  5. Exit friction: return requests trigger safety/legal framing.

Replace the list text with your exact observed sequence if you have dated emails, invoices, or documented calls.

What you should demand in writing (before shipping)

  • Objective test list: continuity, load/voltage-drop, contact resistance, insulation integrity (as applicable).
  • Timeline: a defined completion window and what triggers extensions.
  • Return policy: how to retrieve your property if you stop authorization.
  • Parts sourcing: whether terminals/housings are new OEM-spec, and traceable supplier info.
  • Work authorization boundaries: what “inspection” includes and what is irreversible.
