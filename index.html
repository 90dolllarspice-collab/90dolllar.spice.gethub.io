Key risks

What creates consumer exposure in custody-based specialty services.

The core risk: possession without easy exit

Once your harness is shipped, the business controls the asset, the timeline, and the information flow. If irreversible steps begin early, return “as received” may no longer be possible, and consumer leverage drops sharply.

Why this matters Healthy repair models define scope, cost, timeline, and exit terms before irreversible work begins.

What to watch for

  • Inspection required before any pricing
  • No measurable test criteria described
  • Time-based “cycles” instead of deliverables
  • Deposits + long queue + vague completion windows
  • Escalation when property is requested back

How the model works

Mechanics that create leverage for the shop after shipment.

1) Price becomes flexible after custody

Pricing presented after “inspection” can be adjusted to urgency and fear of replacement scarcity. Options appear once the harness is already under exclusive control.

2) Time is sold as proof of quality

Multi-step internal phases can normalize delay and reduce auditability. When outcomes are hard to measure, “duration” becomes the product.

3) Sunk-cost lock-in

If irreversible steps happen early, customers face an unattractive choice: continue paying or abandon the core. This is how cancellations collapse.

4) Escalation at exit

Many custody-based models escalate only when return is demanded—safety framing, liability talk, or attorney language—because exit threatens possession.

Before you ship

Questions that prevent “no-exit” outcomes.

Non-negotiables to request in writing

✓
Clear scope before irreversible work What steps occur during “inspection,” and what steps are irreversible?
✓
Objective test criteria Continuity? Contact resistance? Voltage drop under load? What metrics define “pass”?
✓
Exit terms If you withdraw authorization, what exactly is owed and how fast must the property be returned?
✓
Timeline range with triggers What conditions extend the timeline, and what is the maximum expected window?
✓
Parts sourcing transparency New parts vs reclaimed; documented supplier sources; traceability where possible.

Fast rule

If pricing, timeline, and exit terms are not defined until after your harness is in their possession, assume you are entering a high-friction custody model.

Practical tip
Photograph the harness from all angles, record connector counts, label IDs, and any markings. Save packing photos and tracking.

FAQ

Short answers to common defenses and sales framing.

“Every harness is unique, so we can’t quote.”
Platform harnesses are standardized. Condition varies. Reputable shops quote ranges using labor matrices (connector count, zones, known failures), then refine after inspection. “No quote at all until custody” is a business choice.
“Our process takes time because safety.”
Safety is validated by measurable tests (contact resistance, voltage drop under load, thermal rise, retention). Time alone is not a safety metric. Ask what tests define “safe” and what numbers they consider acceptable.
“We don’t do repairs; we do restorations.”
Those are marketing categories. What matters is whether the work follows accepted electrical practices, uses appropriate materials, and is validated with objective testing.
“We don’t need warranties because we do it right.”
Any manufactured or serviced electrical assembly can fail. A warranty is a risk allocation decision. If there is no warranty, understand exactly what recourse exists if the harness is returned nonfunctional.

Document everything

How to protect yourself if you ever need to dispute custody, scope, or timeline.

Before shipping

  • Photo/video the harness, connectors, and tags
  • Document serials/IDs, connector count, and visible damage
  • Record packing photos and shipping label/tracking
  • Save all quoted terms in writing (email is fine)

During service

  • Keep a dated log of every contact attempt and response
  • Request test results with numbers, not adjectives
  • Confirm any scope changes in writing before approving
  • Save invoices, deposit receipts, and authorization forms
